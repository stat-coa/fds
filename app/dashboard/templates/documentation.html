{% blocktrans %}A food dollar represents a NT$100 expenditure on domestically produced food by Taiwan consumers. The food dollar is allocated to expenditures on each of the various food commodities sold in proportions that represent their share of annual sales in the domestic market. See the The Survey of Family Income and Expenditure page for information on Family expenditures and Agricultural Trade for information on the import share of Agricultural food.{% endblocktrans %}


{% blocktrans %}The Food Dollar Series divides the food dollar into a set of input-component values. There are three primary series—the marketing bill series, the industry group series, and the primary factor series. For each primary Food Dollar Series, the combined input-component values are equal to the NT$100 output-market value. The three series represent distinct perspectives on the sources of market value for the combined annual food dollar expenditures.{% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Marketing bill series"%}

{% blocktrans %}The marketing bill series is based on sales proceeds. Proceeds from each food dollar expenditure are divided into two sub-components of market value:{% endblocktrans %}

  • {% blocktrans %}“Farm share” measures proceeds of farm commodity sales tied to a food dollar expenditure and sold to nonfarm establishments. Farm share does not include sales of farm commodities that are incorporated into other commodities and resold to a domestic farm industry—this eliminates double counting.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% blocktrans %}“Marketing bill” is the market value added to farm commodities embodied in a food dollar expenditure, measured as NT$1 minus the farm share.{% endblocktrans %}

{% blocktrans with year=objects.year farmshare=marketing_data.FarmShare marketingshare=marketing_data.MarketingShare %}For calendar year {{ year }}, the farm share was {{ farmshare }}% of each food New Taiwan dollar expenditure and the marketing bill was {{ marketingshare }}% accounting for the remainder.{% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Value Added Component Series"%}: {% blocktrans %}For establishments contributing to the Taiwwan food supply, value added for an establishment equals the proceeds from the sale of outputs minus the outlays for commodities purchased from other establishments. The sum of value added by all establishments that contribute to total food dollar purchases equals NT$1. The industry group and primary factor series are based on this value-added concept.{% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Industry group series"%}

{% blocktrans %}Industry groups are establishments grouped together by type of product or service provided. Twelve industry groupings are examined based on the importance of their contributions to the market value of food. For subcontracting establishments—those not classified into one of the 12 industry groups—their value-added contributions are allocated across the 12 industry groups in proportion to the value of goods or services provided to each industry group. The 12 industry groups are:{% endblocktrans %}

  • {% trans "Agribusiness"%}—{% blocktrans %}all establishments producing farm inputs (except those described in other industry groups) such as seed, fertilizers, farm machinery, farm services, and all subcontracting establishments.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% trans "Farm production"%}—{% blocktrans %}all establishments classified within the agriculture, forestry, fishing, and hunting industries.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% trans "Food processing"%}—{% blocktrans %}all establishments classified within the food and beverage manufacturing industries, and all subcontracting establishments.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% trans "Packaging"%}—{% blocktrans %}all establishments classified within the packaging, container, and print manufacturing industries, and all subcontracting establishments.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% trans "Transportation"%}—{% blocktrans %}all establishments classified within the freight services industries and all subcontracting establishments.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% trans "Wholesale trade"%}—{% blocktrans %}all non-retail establishments reselling products to other establishments for the purpose of contributing to the U.S. food supply and all subcontracting establishments.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% trans "Retail trade"%}—{% blocktrans %}all food retailing and related establishments and all subcontracting establishments.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% trans "Food service"%}—{% blocktrans %}all eating, drinking, and related establishments as well as all subcontracting establishments.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% trans "Energy"%}—{% blocktrans %}oil and coal mining, gas and electric utilities, refineries, related establishments, and all subcontracting establishments.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% trans "Finance and insurance"%}—{% blocktrans %}all financial services and insurance carrier establishments and all subcontracting establishments.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% trans "Advertising"%}—{% blocktrans %}all advertising services and related establishments and all subcontracting establishments.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% trans "Legal and accounting"%}—{% blocktrans %}establishments providing legal, accounting, and bookkeeping services, as well as all subcontracting establishments.{% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Primary factor series"%}

{% blocktrans %}Primary factors are assets employed by establishments to use or transform products purchased from other establishments (intermediate inputs) to produce and market a different product. These assets add market value to the purchased intermediate products. In the food dollar accounts, value added is recorded as income to primary factors as follows:{% endblocktrans %}

  • {% blocktrans %}Domestic hired labor is allocated Compensation(Comp) for services on behalf of a domestic establishment that directs sales towards fulfilling the supply of food to the U.S. market.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% trans "Operating Surplus(OS)"%}—{% blocktrans %}machinery, equipment, structures, natural resources, product inventory, and other tangible or intangible assets are allocated compensation to various owners for services on behalf of a domestic establishment that direct sales to the U.S. food supply.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% trans "Consumption of Fixed Capital(CFC)"%}—{% blocktrans %}the current replacement costs on assets, distinct from the historical cost on business accounting. Hence CFC is imputed by estimating the capital stock according to GFCF time series and the declining balance method, instead of the financial reports.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% trans "Net taxes(Taxes)"%}—{% blocktrans %}excise, sales, property, and severance taxes (less subsidies), customs duties, and non-tax fees are levied by government independently of establishment dispersals to primary factor owners. Taxes tied to outlays for domestic labor and capital (such as income-based taxes) are not reported separately but are included in the primary domestic factor returns.{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% trans "Imports"%}—{% blocktrans %}international assets are compensated for their services on behalf of non-domestic establishments whose products are imported and are directed to supplying Taiwan-produced food to the Taiwan market. Because these services cannot be traced to their primary factor sources, their values are measured in the form of the products imported, and their value added is assigned to industry groups with which they are associated.{% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Data sources and estimation methodology"%}

{% blocktrans %}Statistics Office of Council of Agriculture(COA) compiled Food Dollar Series based on national input-output:{% endblocktrans %}

  • {% trans "Annual IO tables"%} {% blocktrans %}Data published every year by the Directorate General of Budget, Accounting and Statistics(DGBAS).{% endblocktrans %}
  • {% trans "Benchmark IO tables"%} {% blocktrans with year=objects.year%}Data from the 1981, 1986, …, 2011 and {{ year }} detailed Taiwan benchmark IO accounts also published by the Directorate General of Budget, Accounting and Statistics(DGBAS).{% endblocktrans %}
